## Learning Summary

The core idea of this notebook is that note-taking should function as a **tool for thinking** rather than a passive archive for storage. Evergreen notes represent a shift from transient, source-oriented capture to **atomic, concept-oriented insight development**. By writing in one's own words (utilizing the **generation effect**) and connecting ideas topographically rather than hierarchically, knowledge workers can facilitate the organic emergence of new insights. This system directly supports the `[[wiki/synthesis/digital-garden-ethos|Digital Garden Ethos]]` by treating knowledge as a living network that requires active tending and cultivation over time.

## Key Ideas

* **Atomicity:** Each evergreen note should focus on a single, self-contained idea to facilitate flexible recombination.
* **Concept-Orientation:** Notes are organized by concepts (ideas) rather than by the books, authors, or projects they originated from.
* **Dense Linking:** Explicit associations between notes create an "associative ontology" that surfaces unexpected connections.
* **Generation Effect:** The cognitive benefit of creating one's own material—writing notes in one's own words—results in better memory and understanding than passive reading.
* **Incremental Writing:** Evergreen notes act as "solid ground," allowing for smooth, bottom-up composition of larger manuscripts by connecting existing blocks of thought.
* **Tending vs. Archiving:** Note-taking is an ongoing learning journey; note archival (storing information) is a misconception that often fails to force active thinking.

## Important Claims

* **"Better note-taking" misses the point; what matters is "better thinking"**.
* **Evergreen note titles are like APIs:** Well-titled notes become abstractions that can be referenced as handles for complex ideas.
* **Note-taking is the "real" work:** The process of developing the insight is the fundamental unit of knowledge work, not just the final publication.
* **Associative ontologies are superior to hierarchical taxonomies:** Links that cut across categories prevent information from being siloed.
* **Digital tools democratize the practice:** While possible on paper, digital apps lower the barrier for everyday practical use.
